# Carburetor cleaning

- Disassemble all four carbs and note jet sizes and positions before mixing parts.
- Ultrasonic cleaning is the best method for VF750 carb bodies.
- Clear all jets, passages, and pilot circuits with compressed air.
- Replace float valve needles if they are stuck or worn.
- If a bowl stays dry even though fuel reaches the rack, inspect the float valve, seat filter, float height, bowl vent, and inlet rail before changing jet sizes.
- If fuel is present but one cylinder blues the pipe and shows a white plug, especially after an unsuccessful idle-drop adjustment, inspect that carb's pilot/low-throttle circuit carefully.
- Reference video: [Honda VF750 Magna Carb Clean - TheMotorcycleMD](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=52k8LaD3q4Y)

## Checking CV pistons, needles, and diaphragms

The piston and needle in a CV carb should not behave like a free mechanical slide. It should lift smoothly with a finger, without binding or cocking, and return smoothly. All four pistons should feel roughly the same.

Quick check:

1. Remove the airbox lid or otherwise get access to the carb inlets.
2. Gently lift the piston and needle with a finger.
3. Release it and watch how it returns.
4. Repeat on all four carbs and compare speed and feel.

Normal behavior:

- the piston lifts smoothly;
- there are no steps, sticking spots, or side-loading;
- it returns evenly when released;
- all four pistons behave similarly;
- the movement is not rock-hard, but it is not completely free either.

Vacuum damping check:

1. Lift the piston.
2. Cover the vacuum hole/passage that controls the piston with your finger.
3. Release the piston.
4. With the hole covered, it should drop noticeably more slowly.

If one piston drops much faster than the others, look for a torn, cracked, or incorrectly seated diaphragm. If one piston is much tighter, look for dirt, a burr, a distorted cap, swollen rubber, a bent needle, or mixed-up parts.

Diaphragm check:

1. Remove the top carb cover.
2. Carefully lift out the spring and piston/diaphragm assembly.
3. Inspect the diaphragm against light.
4. Check the outer lip: it should be soft and sit cleanly in the carb-body groove.
5. During assembly, align the diaphragm tab/mark with its locating feature.

Bad signs:

- cracks, pinholes, or tears;
- hardened or swollen rubber;
- diaphragm lip popping out of the groove;
- the cap pinching the diaphragm off-center.

Needle check:

- the needle should be straight;
- it should not have a wear step, heavy scoring, or rub marks;
- retainers, washers, springs, and holders should match the manual;
- the needle should not catch the emulsion tube as the piston moves;
- do not mix needles between carbs if front and rear carbs use different parts.

Symptoms of piston or diaphragm problems:

- one cylinder is weak under load even though it works at idle;
- one plug looks different from the others;
- one cylinder overheats its pipe;
- throttle response is delayed or flat;
- sync appears set, but the engine still runs unevenly.

## How to clear a plugged pilot circuit

A plugged pilot circuit is not fixed by spraying cleaner through the carb mouth. Remove the carb rack, remove the pilot jet, and prove that every small passage is open.

Procedure:

1. Remove the carb rack from the bike.
2. Remove the float bowl from the problem carb.
3. Remove the pilot/slow jet.
4. Before removing the pilot mixture screw, gently turn it in until it lightly seats and record the number of turns. Then remove the screw, spring, washer, and O-ring.
5. Inspect the pilot jet against light: the bore must be open.
6. Flush the jet with carb cleaner and compressed air. Do not use a steel needle; it can enlarge the metering hole.
7. If you need mechanical help, use a copper strand, soft wire, or fishing line.
8. Flush the pilot-jet passage, mixture-screw passage, and the small outlet ports at the throttle-plate edge.
9. Spray carb cleaner into the pilot-jet passage: it should exit from the mixture-screw hole and the small ports by the throttle plate.
10. Repeat compressed-air blowing until flow is clear.
11. Inspect the pilot-screw O-ring. Replace it if it is hard, torn, flattened, or missing.
12. Reassemble the carb and return the pilot screw to the model/year-specific baseline.

Signs the circuit is still not clean:

- the pilot screw barely changes idle behavior;
- one cylinder stays cold at idle or too hot under load;
- the engine only runs on choke/enrichment;
- rpm hangs after warm-up;
- one plug stays white even after proper sync.

Ultrasonic cleaning helps, but it does not replace passage verification. After the ultrasonic bath, still prove every passage with carb cleaner and compressed air.
